Sub-Chapter 5 Eligibility Excluded Service

Sub-Chapter 5. Eligibility Excluded Service

Certain periods of active-duty service are not qualifying for the purposes of establishing eligibility for chapter 33 benefits. For example, periods of service obligated by a Loan Repayment Program (LRP), or a commission as a result of a ROTC program under Title 10 USC 2107 (b), or graduation from a Service Academy do not qualify as active duty. Therefore, these service periods do not count toward eligibility for chapter 33 benefits.

There are 4 distinct categories in regard to excluded or service periods that do not qualify for Chapter 33:

5.01 Periods of service not considered to be active duty

5.02 Periods of active duty excluded from eligibility determinations

5.03 Periods of active duty deducted for eligibility

5.04 Periods of active duty excluded from eligibility determinations

5.05 United States Coast Guard Academy Graduates

 

5.01 Periods of Service Not Considered to be Active Duty

a. Non-Qualifying Periods

For the Post-9/11 GI Bill, qualifying active duty service does not include any period during which an individual:

  • Was assigned to a civilian institution for a course of education which was substantially the same as established courses offered to civilians.
  • Served as a cadet or midshipman at one of the service academies.
  • Served under the provisions of section 12103 (d) of Title 10 pursuant to an enlistment in the Army National Guard, Air National Guard or Reserve components.
  • Was called up to active duty from a reserve component of the Armed Forces under Title 10 and it was under a section other than 688, 12301(a), 12301(d), 12301(g), 12301(h), 12302, 12304, 12304a, or 12304b.
  • Was called up to active duty under Title 32 that was NOT under Title 32 502(f) for the purpose of responding to a national emergency declared by the President and supported by Federal Funds.
  • Served full time in the National Guard under Title 32 for a purpose other than organizing, administering, recruiting, instructing, or training.
  • Was called up to active duty from the Coast Guard Reserve under a section other than section 3713 of Title 14 (formerly section 712).

Scenario

Example 1: A Cadet attends West Point and submits a DD-214 with dates showing October 1, 2006, through October 2, 2010. These dates would be entered as a non-qualifying period of service as those periods do not count as active duty for eligibility towards chapter 33 benefits. As a result, no eligibility would be established in DGI.

 5.02 Periods of Active Duty Excluded from Eligibility Determinations

a. Excluded Service

For the Post-9/11 GI Bill, certain periods of active duty service are prohibited from being used to establish eligibility. These periods include:

  • Obligated service pursuant to being commissioned through a Reserve Officers Training Corps (ROTC) scholarship program under 10 U.S.C. 2107(b). The obligated period is four years.
  • Obligated service pursuant to a commission upon graduating from a Service Academy. The obligated period is five years.

NOTE: The obligated service will only be deducted for a Coast Guard claimant if his or her active duty commitment commences on or after January 4, 2011.

  • Obligated service pursuant to the repayment of education loans under 10 U.S.C. 109 (unless service was in the Coast Guard or Marine Corps). The obligated period is three years.
  • Any service resulting from defective enlistment agreements, induction based on the individual being a minor, or an erroneous enlistment or induction.

Scenario

Example ROTC:  A veteran is discharged from service with a R.O.T.C. obligated period. That period of R.O.T.C. must be excluded from his/her chapter 33 benefits eligibility. The record reflects service on active duty as follows:

September 1, 2004 to September 2, 2009. Since four years of service must be excluded. The processor will enter the service dates from September 1, 2004, to September 2, 2009, and then enter September 1, 2004, to August 31, 2008, as excluded service. Eligibility does not begin until the day after their four-year commitment has been completed. The eligible period of service for chapter 33 benefits would be September 1, 2008, to September 2, 2009.

Example Service Academy:

A West Point graduate, graduates on May 27, 2003, was commissioned on May 27, 2003, and served through May 26, 2008. The individual's academy commitment ended on May 27, 2008. The five year excluded period runs from May 27, 2003 through May 26, 2008. The first date eligibility for a period of qualifying service may be counted is May 27, 2008.

Title 10 USC 4348(a)(2)(B) for USMA, Title 10 USC 6959(a)(2)(B) for United States Naval Academy,  and Title 1010 USC 9348(a)(2)(B) for United States Air Force Academy.“will serve on active duty for at least five years immediately after such appointment”.

5.03 Periods of Active Duty Deducted for Eligibility Determinations

a. Deducted Service

The term “Entry Level and Skill Training” means the following :

Army:  Basic Combat Training and Advanced Individual Training or One Station Unit Training

Enlisted Soldiers attend Basic Training (BCT):

Either combined with Advanced Individual Training (AIT) also known as One Station Unit Training (OSUT) or AIT occurs at another location to earn a military occupational skill (MOS)

Additional skill identifier or other training should not be identified as training. (i.e., Airborne School, Defense Language Institute, Ranger or Special Forces Training…).  This time is fully creditable under chapter 33 unless performed by a member of the National Guard and may or may not be creditable service

Navy: Recruit Training (or Boot Camp) and Skill Training (or so-called "A" School)

Enlisted Sailors attend Recruit Training or Boot Camp (RTC):

Many attend advanced skill training (apprenticeship) or “A” School to earn a “rating”

Others go directly into the fleet without additional training serving as an airmen, fireman or seaman

Air Force:  Basic Military Training and Technical Training

Enlisted Airmen attend Basic Military Training (BMT):

Attend Technical Training to earn their Air Force Specialty Code (AFSC) which is similar to a Navy “Rating” or the Army and Marines MOS job identification

Marine Corps:  Recruit Training and Marine Corps Training (or School of Infantry Training)

United States Marine Corps (USMC) enlistees enter Boot Camp:

Individual will attend either School of Infantry (SOI) or Marine Combat Training (MCT)

Subsequent on job skill training or MOS training should not be identified as training. This time is fully creditable under chapter 33

Coast Guard: Basic Training and Skill Training (or so-called "A" School)

Enlisted Guardians attend Basic Training:

Similar to the Navy, attend “A” School to earn a rating

NOTE: Only Basic Training should be identified for those entering service prior to January 4, 2011.  PL 111-377 added “A” School to the definition of entry level and skill training for Coast Guard.

For the Post-9/11 GI Bill, periods of Entry Level and Skill Training must be deducted for entitlement calculations when an individual does not complete at least 24 months of active duty service. Those periods may be deducted if the individual served less than 30 months of active duty service (see benefit level chart)

Example:

Suzie served a total of 27 months (810 days) of aggregate service. Of those, four months (120 days) was entry-level training.  Based on 810 days of total aggregate service, she would qualify at the 80% level.

Excluding her entry-level training, she served a total of 690 days. This would qualify her at the 70% level.

Example 2:

Lynn served a total of 7 months (210 days) of aggregate service. Of those, two months (60 days) were entry-level training.

At what level would VA pay benefits?

Seven months (210 days) of service would normally qualify at the 60% level, however, 60 of Lynn’s 210 days were entry level training.

Entry level and skill training is excluded at 70% or less.

As a result, Lynn only has five months (150 days) of qualifying service.

Lynn would qualify at the 50% level.

5.04 ROTC: Scholarship vs. Non-scholarship Contracts

a. Determining Commissioning Statute

A commission received after successful completion of the ROTC program may be under one of three different statutory authorities: section 2014, 2107(b), or 2107a of Title 10. Only a commission under the authority of 2107(b) results in an obligated exclusion period under chapter 33.

To determine the commissioning statute, processors should refer to the “Military Source Accession Code” field, located in the “Excluded Obligated Periods of Service” section of the Veterans Information System (VIS) report. The following table contains the accession codes correlated to ROTC, the commissioning statute of each, and whether an exclusion applies.

If conflicting information is reported, such as exclusion data displayed with an accession code other than one listed above or any other indicator ROTC may be an issue, further development may be required to accurately determine if an exclusion applies.

b. Evaluating ROTC Scholarship Contracts. 

When further development is required to accurately determine the applicability of an exclusion and appropriate exclusion dates, it may be necessary to evaluate the ROTC Scholarship contract and/or orders relating to the commission. The title at the top of each ROTC contract will state whether it is a ROTC Non-scholarship Cadet Contract or a ROTC Scholarship Cadet Contract. ROTC Non-scholarship Cadet Contracts are always under section 2104 of title 10, U.S.C. While the contract may require an individual to fulfill an active-duty service obligation upon receiving his or her commission, this active-duty service should NOT be excluded from qualifying service under chapter 33.

ROTC Scholarship Cadet Contracts can be under either section 2107 or 2107a of title 10, U.S.C. The contract should indicate whether it is under section 2107 or section 2107a. 

  • If section 2107a indicated, active-duty service fulfilling the individual’s contract obligation it should NOT be excluded from qualifying service under chapter 33. 
  • If section 2107 indicated, the contract is under paragraph (b) of section 2107, and active-duty service fulfilling the individual’s contract obligation must be excluded from qualifying service under chapter 33.

c. Determining excluded period dates

Because chapter 33 is a title 38 benefit, the VA is responsible for prescription of regulations which govern administration of the benefit. Accordingly, VA has determined a commission under the authority of 2107(b) of Title 10 will incur a four-year exclusion period under chapter 33. The VIS application currently reports exclusion dates based on data reported by DoD, including the accession source code, in the “Excluded Obligated Periods of Service” table. The dates which appear in this table are derived data and not authoritative. The reported dates may be used as a guide for determining the exclusion period, but may contain clearly erroneous data. While the VA must rely on DoD data to make these determinations, the DoD does not determine applicability of an exclusion, nor the dates of an exclusion. VA is the sole authority for determining the applicable dates for the exclusion period, based on facts found.

Determine the exclusion period when 2107(b) is an issue by using the following process:

1. Commissioned as an officer in regular component – The exclusion period for a solider commissioned as an officer in a regular component begins on the entrance on duty date of the service period which began due to commission and ends after four years. Service begins accruing towards eligibility on the four-year anniversary of commission.

A solider who is member of a Guard or Reserve component while in the ROTC program, and who subsequently receives a commission as an officer in a regular component and has a period of delayed entry between their commission date and entrance on duty in the regular component, should have their exclusion period begin on the entrance on duty date in the regular component and end after four years. Service begins accruing towards eligibility on the four-year anniversary of their entrance on duty.

Example 1: A solider receives a commission under 2107(b) to serve as an officer in a regular component on May 23, 2019. They were a member of a Reserve component from September 27, 2017, to May 22, 2019, while participating in the ROTC program. They entered the regular component on May 23, 2019. The exclusion period is May 23, 2019, to May 22, 2023. Service begins accruing on May 23, 2019.

Example 2: A solider receives a commission under 2107(b) to serve as an officer in a regular component on May 23, 2019. They were a member of a Reserve component from September 27, 2017, to September 20, 2019, while participating in the ROTC program. They entered the regular component on September 21, 2019, after a period of delayed entry. The exclusion period is September 21, 2019, to September 20, 2023. Service begins accruing on September 21, 2023.

Example 3: A solider enters the regular component as an enlisted member. Subsequently, they enter the ROTC program while on duty and receive a commission under 2107(b) to serve as an officer in the same component.  They entered the regular component on July 1, 2014, participated in the ROTC program while on duty, and were commissioned June 14, 2016. The exclusion period is June 14, 2016, to June 13, 2020. Service performed prior to June 14, 2016, and service performed on or after June 14, 2020, accrue towards the eligibility.

2. Commissioned as an officer in a Guard or Reserve component – The exclusion period for a solider commissioned as an officer in a Guard or Reserve component begins on the commission date and ends after four years. Any active-duty service performed under a qualifying section during the four-year period must be excluded. Active duty performed under a qualifying section on or after the four-year anniversary will accrue towards eligibility.

Example 1: A solider receives a commission under 2107(b) to serve as an officer in a Guard or Reserve component on June 5, 2020. They were a member of a Guard or Reserve component from September 20, 2017, to June 4, 2020, while participating in the ROTC program. They became an officer in the Guard or Reserve on June 5, 2020. Any active-duty service performed under a qualifying section from June 5, 2020, to June 4, 2024, must be excluded. Active duty performed under a qualifying section on or after June 5, 2024, will accrue towards eligibility.

5.05 United States Coast Guard Academy Graduates

a. Background: The Post-9/11 Veterans Educational Assistance Improvements Act of 2010 (Public Law 111-377) mandates that an Active Duty Service Obligation (ADSO) incurred pursuant to commissioning following graduation from the United States Coast Guard Academy cannot be used to establish benefit eligibility. The provision was effective on January 4, 2011, the date of enactment. The new requirement applies to individuals entering into agreements on service in the Coast Guard on or after that date. Agreements for the Coast Guard Academy are usually signed the first day of entrance into the academy or two weeks prior to the first day of entrance.

b. Procedures: Review the information available in TIMS or VIS to see if you can reasonably infer the date on which a claimant would have signed a “Statement of Acceptance and Obligation.” If the "Statement of Acceptance and Obligation" is not available in the file, please follow local procedures and develop with the USCG point-of-contact, the claimant, and any other available resources to obtain the information.

  • If the date of the signed agreement is prior to January 4, 2011, process the claim without the exclusion of service following graduation from the Coast Guard Academy.
  • If the date of the signed agreement is on or after January 4, 2011, leave the ADSO as an Excluded Period in the DGI. That period of service is not qualifying for determinations of Post-9/11 GI Bill benefit eligibility.

For example: If an individual’s obligated service began on June 15, 2013, following graduation from a four-year degree program at the Coast Guard Academy, then they would have signed their statement well before January 4, 2011. Likewise, if an individual’s obligated service begins on June 5, 2017, then they logically would have signed their statement well after January 4, 2011. If there is ambiguous information regarding when a cadet may have signed an agreement or the date cannot be safely estimated, then the VCE should develop to the Coast Guard point-of-contact for the date the statement was signed.
